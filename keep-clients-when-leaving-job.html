The real cost of leaving

What you actually lose when you walk out the door.

You've spent months — maybe years — building relationships with your clients. They book with you, specifically. They come back because they trust you, not because they love the salon, the gym, or the clinic. That trust is yours. But when you leave a job without a plan, those relationships don't automatically come with you.

Most employers know this. Some contracts explicitly prohibit you from "soliciting" former clients. Most booking systems don't hand you an export of your client database on your last day. Your client list lives in their CRM, their app, their phone system — not yours.

For a hairdresser, losing even five regular clients could mean thousands of pounds a year in lost income. For a personal trainer or physio, a handful of loyal clients might represent the majority of your revenue. This isn't just inconvenient — it's financially significant.

What happens without a plan

The cost of leaving unprepared

  • Clients try to rebook with "you" — and find out you've left, from your replacement
  • No way to tell clients your new location without going through old employer
  • Contracts may restrict you from reaching out directly to former clients
  • You start from scratch building a new client base — losing months of income
  • Clients who liked you still go to your old workplace out of convenience
The awkward truth

Why employers think they own your client relationships.

In many industries — hair, fitness, health, education — the business genuinely believes it owns the client relationship, because the client found you through them. The salon brought in the footfall. The gym provided the space. The clinic did the marketing. So when you leave, they expect the client to stay.

And legally, they often have a point. Non-solicitation clauses are common and enforceable. Even without a written contract, poaching clients is considered bad form in many industries, and can lead to disputes.

But here's the thing: if you've genuinely built a personal relationship with a client, and that client wants to follow you to your new location — that's their choice. Sidedoor doesn't let you do anything aggressive. It lets you quietly make yourself available to clients who want to find you. If they know your word, they can reach you. If they don't — nothing happens.
