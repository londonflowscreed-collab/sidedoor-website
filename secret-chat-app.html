What privacy actually means

Most messaging apps are private. Few are discreet.

There's a difference between an app that encrypts your messages and an app that nobody can tell you're using. Signal and WhatsApp handle the first part well. But they can't help you with the second. If your partner, your employer, or anyone else glances at your phone, they can see Signal. They can see WhatsApp. They know those conversations exist.

Truly private messaging means the app itself is invisible. It means conversations don't linger once they've been read. It means there's no public profile, no notification banner that gives the game away, no distinctive icon on your home screen. That's a much higher bar — and it's what Sidedoor is built to clear.

Sidedoor isn't trying to replace Signal for activists or journalists. It's designed for a specific use case: professionals who need a private, direct channel with clients, completely separate from their employer and their personal life.

How it's different

Not Signal. Not WhatsApp. Something else entirely.

Signal protects your message content with end-to-end encryption. WhatsApp does too. But both apps are still visible — on your home screen, in your app library, in your notification shade. Anyone who knows you have Signal might wonder why. Anyone who sees a message from an unknown number on WhatsApp might ask.

Sidedoor operates on a different axis. It's not primarily about who can read your messages — it's about who knows the conversation is happening at all. The app itself is hidden. The contact appears as someone else. The messages disappear. It's designed for situations where you need plausible deniability, not just cryptographic security.
